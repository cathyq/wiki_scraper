It is one of the most influential and widely read [[book review]] publications in the industry.<ref name=cspan>{{cite web|title=Inside ''The New York Times Book Review''|url=http://www.c-span.org/video/?195760-1/inside-new-york-times-book-review|publisher=[[C-SPAN]]|accessdate=12 April 2015|date=17 October 2006|quote=A behind-the-scenes tour of the offices of the New York Times Book Review showed how an issue is created.\u2002Editor Sam Tanenhaus guided the tour through the editorial and production process of review while staff members described their various responsibilities.\u2002Included were selecting and rejecting books; choosing reviewers for books; fact checking and editing the review; composing the layout design; creating headlines, blurbs, and artwork; and selecting and editing letters from readers.}}</ref> The offices are located near [[Times Square]] in [[New York City]].\n\n==Overview==\n\n''The New York Times'' has published a book review section [[1896 in literature|since October 10, 1896]], announcing:\n\n{{quote|We begin today the publication of a Supplement which contains reviews of new books .. and other interesting matter .. associated with news of the day.|October 10, 1896|<ref>[http://timesmachine.nytimes.com/browser/1896/10/10/ ''The New York Times''], October 10, 1896. Inaugural book review issue (announced on page 4, column 1)</ref>}}\n\nThe target audience is an intelligent, general-interest adult reader.<ref name=cspan/> The ''Times'' publishes two versions each week, one with a cover price sold via subscription, bookstores and newsstands; the other with no cover price included as an insert in each Sunday edition of the ''Times'' (the copies are otherwise identical).\n\nEach week the NYTBR receives 750 to 1000 books from authors and publishers in the mail, of which 20 to 30 are chosen for review.<ref name=cspan/> Books are selected by the \"preview editors\" who read over 1,500 advance galleys a year.<ref name=charney>{{cite web |title=Inside the NYT Book Review: \u2018How I Write\u2019 Interviews Sam Tanenhaus |url=http://www.thedailybeast.com/articles/2012/08/08/inside-the-nyt-book-review-how-i-write-interviews-sam-tanenhaus.html |author=Noah Charney |date=August 8, 2012 |work=The Daily Beast |accessdate=August 9, 2012}}</ref> The selection process is based on finding books that are important and notable, as well as discovering new authors whose books stand above the crowd.<ref name=cspan/> Self-published books are generally not reviewed as a matter of policy.<ref name=cspan/> Books not selected for review are stored in a \"discard room\" and then sold.<ref name=cspan/> {{as of|2006}}, [[Barnes & Noble]] arrived about once a month to purchase the contents of the discard room, and the proceeds are then donated by NYTBR to charities.<ref name=cspan/> Books that are actually reviewed are usually donated to the reviewer.<ref name=cspan/>\n\nAs of 2015, all review critics are freelance, the NYTBR does not have staff critics.<ref name=paulfaq>{{cite web |url=http://www.nytimes.com/2016/01/01/insider/answering-the-most-frequent-questions-about-the-book-review.html |title=Answering the Most Frequent Questions About the Book Review |work=New York Times Book Review |author=Pamela Paul |date=January 1, 2016 |accessdate=January 2, 2016}}</ref> In prior years, the NYTBR did have in-house critics, or a mix of in-house and freelance.<ref name=cspan/> For freelance critics, they are assigned an in-house \"preview editor\" who works with them in creating the final review.<ref name=cspan/> Freelance critics might be employees of ''The New York Times'' whose main duties are in other departments.<ref name=paulfaq/> They also include professional literary critics, novelists, academics and artists who write reviews for the NYTBR on a regular basis.<ref name=paulfaq/> \n\nOther duties on staff include a number of Senior Editors and a Chief Editor; a team of Copy Editors; a Letter Pages Editor who reads letters to the editor; columnists who write weekly columns, such as the \"Paperback Row\" column; a Production Editor; a web and Internet publishing division; and other jobs.<ref name=cspan/> In addition to the magazine there is an Internet site that offers additional content, including audio interviews with authors, called the \"Book Review Podcast\".<ref name=cspan/>\n\nThe book review publishes each week the widely cited and influential [[The New York Times Best Seller list|''New York Times''' Best Seller list]], which is created by the editors of the ''Times'' \"News Surveys\" department.<ref name=pierleoni>{{cite web |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20120125082356/http://www.sacbee.com/2012/01/22/4203094/best-sellers-lists-how-they-work.html |archivedate=January 25, 2012 |url=http://www.sacbee.com/2012/01/22/4203094/best-sellers-lists-how-they-work.html |title=Best-sellers lists: How they work and who they (mostly) work for |work=The Sacramento Bee |author=Allen Pierleoni |date=Jan 22, 2012 |accessdate=August 13, 2014 |deadurl=yes}}</ref>\n\n[[Pamela Paul]] was named Senior Editor in spring 2013. [[Sam Tanenhaus]] was Senior Editor from the spring of 2004 to spring 2013.\n\n==Best Books of the Year and Notable Books {{anchor|notable}}<!--anchor used in redirects-->==\nEach year, around the beginning of December, a \"100 Notable Books of the Year\" list is published.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.nytimes.com/2010/12/05/books/review/100-notable-books-2010.html|title=HOLIDAY GIFT GUIDE; 100 Notable Books of 2010|date=5 December 2010|work=The New York Times|page=28|accessdate=7 January 2011}} (page has links to previous years also)</ref> It contains fiction and non-fiction titles of books previously reviewed, 50 of each. From the list of 100, 10 books are awarded the \"Best Books of the Year\" title, 5 each of fiction and non-fiction. Other year-end lists include the Best Illustrated Children's Books, in which 10 books are chosen by a panel of judges.\n\n==Studies==\nIn 2010, [[Stanford]] professors Alan Sorenson and [[Jonah Berger]] published a study examining the effect on book sales from positive or negative reviews in the ''New York Times Book Review''.<ref name=sorenson>Alan Sorenson, Jonah Berger. [http://mktsci.journal.informs.org/content/29/5/815.abstract \"Positive Effects of Negative Publicity: When Negative Reviews Increase Sales\"]. ''[[Marketing Science (journal)|Marketing Science]]'', Vol. 29, No. 5, September\u2013October 2010, pp. 815\u2013827.</ref><ref name=thai/> They found all books benefited from positive reviews, while popular or well-known authors were negatively impacted by negative reviews.<ref name=sorenson/><ref name=thai/> Lesser-known authors benefited from negative reviews; in other words, bad publicity actually boosted book sales.<ref name=sorenson/><ref name=thai>[http://www.stanforddaily.com/2011/02/23/bad-publicity-may-boost-book-sales/ \"Bad publicity may boost book sales\"], Jenny Thai, ''[[The Stanford Daily]]'', Feb. 23 2011.</ref>\n\nA study published in 2012, by University professor and author [[Roxane Gay]], found that 90 percent of the ''New York Times'' book reviews published in 2011 were of books by white authors.<ref name=gay>{{cite web |url=http://therumpus.net/2012/06/where-things-stand/ |author=Roxane Gay |work=[[The Rumpus]] |title=Where Things Stand |date=June 6, 2012 |accessdate=June 13, 2012}}</ref> Gay said, \"The numbers reflect the overall trend in publishing where the majority of books published are written by white writers.\"<ref name=gay/> At the time of the report, the racial makeup of the United States was 72 percent white.<ref name=gay/>\n\n==References==\n{{Reflist}}\n\n==External links==\n*[http://www.nytimes.com/pages/books/review/ ''The New York Times Book Review''], home page.\n*[http://timesmachine.nytimes.com/browser/1896/10/10/ ''The New York Times''], October 10, 1896.
