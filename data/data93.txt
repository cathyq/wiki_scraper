His mother was of mostly [[English American|English]] and [[Scottish American|Scottish]] origin<ref>[http://www.americanancestors.org/arthur-ochs-sulzberger-jr/ New England Historic Genealogical Society - American Ancestors: #42 Royal Descents, Notable Kin, and Printed Sources: Yankee Ancestors, Mayflower Lines, and Royal Descents and Connections of Arthur Ochs Sulzberger Jr.] by Gary Boyd Roberts; dated December 1, 1999</ref> and his father was of [[Jewish American|Jewish]] origin (both Ashkenazi and Sephardic). His parents divorced when he was five. He was raised in his mother's [[Episcopal Church (United States)|Episcopalian]] faith, but no longer observes the religion.<ref>[http://books.google.com/books?id=AOkCAAAAMBAJ&pg=PA30&dq=sulzberger+episcopalian&hl=en&ei=_w2KTdfEE4nYgQf9wpHLDQ&sa=X&oi=book_result&ct=result&resnum=1&ved=0CC0Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&q=sulzberger%20episcopalian&f=false ''New Times''], New York Magazine, Sept. 30, 1991, p. 30</ref> On May 24, 1975, he married artist and journalist [[Gail Gregg]]. The ceremony was performed by Rev. Katherine Belton, a Presbyterian assistant pastor, in the Greggs' garden in [[Topeka, Kansas]].<ref>''Arthur O. Sulzberger Jr. and Gail Gregg Married'', May 25, 1975, New York Times, p. 51</ref>  In May 2008, they announced plans to end their marriage.<ref>[http://www.nytimes.com/2008/05/10/nyregion/10marriage.html?_r=1&oref=slogin \"Times Publisher and His Wife Separate\"], ''[[The New York Times]]'', May 10, 2008.  Accessed August 10, 2008.</ref>\n\nSulzberger earned a Bachelor of Arts degree in [[political science]] from [[Tufts University]] in 1974.  He was a [[reporter]] with The ''[[Raleigh Times]]'' from 1974 to 1976, and a London correspondent for The [[Associated Press]] from 1976-78. He joined ''The New York Times'' in 1978 as a correspondent in its Washington bureau. He moved to New York as a metro reporter in 1981 and was appointed assistant metro editor later that year. He is also a 1985 graduate of the Harvard Business School's Program for Management Development.\n\nFrom 1983 to 1987, he worked in a variety of business departments, including production and corporate planning. In January 1987, he was named assistant publisher and, a year later, deputy publisher, overseeing the news and business departments. In both capacities, he was involved in planning the ''Times'''s automated color printing and distribution facilities in [[Edison, New Jersey]], and at [[College Point, Queens|College Point, Queens, New York]], as well as the creation of the six-section color newspaper. Sulzberger played a central role in the development of the [[Times Square]] Business Improvement District, officially launched in January 1992, serving as the first chairman of that civic organization. He also helped found and was a two-term chairman of New York City [[Outward Bound]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Robert Miller Named Chairman of NYC Outward Bound Board|url=http://www.nycoutwardbound.org/images/content/0/5/054.pdf|publisher=NYC Outward Bound|accessdate=2014-05-16|date=January 3, 2007}}</ref>\n\nSulzberger has been credited with developing the ''Times'''s internet operations, monetizing digital content, etc., helping to improve the paper's bottom line.<ref>{{cite web|accessdate=September 15, 2008|url=http://www.businesswire.com/portal/site/google/?ndmViewId=news_view&newsId=20050418006138&newsLang=en|title=The New York Times Company Reports NYTimes.com's Record-Breaking Traffic for March|work=The New York Times|date=April 18, 2005}}</ref> The domain \"nytimes.com\" attracted at least 146,000,000 visitors annually by 2008, according to a [[Compete.com]] study. ''The New York Times'' Web site was ranked 59th with more than 20,000,000 unique visitors in March 2009, making it the most visited newspaper site with more than twice as many unique visitors as the next most popular site. The ''Times'' also had the most entries in the list of the 50 most popular newspaper blogs, with 22 of its blogs listed that year.<ref>{{cite web|accessdate=April 22, 2009|url=http://www.businessinsider.com/the-50-most-popular-newspaper-blogs-2009-5|title=The 50 Most Popular Newspaper Blogs|publisher=Business Insider| archiveurl= http://web.archive.org/web/20090510142954/http://www.businessinsider.com/the-50-most-popular-newspaper-blogs-2009-5| archivedate= May 10, 2009 <!--DASHBot-->| deadurl= no}}</ref>\n\n==Personal life==\nSulzberger married [[Gail Gregg]] on May 24, 1975 and divorced in 2008.<ref name=pagesix>{{cite web |url=http://pagesix.com/2014/08/30/nyt-boss-to-marry-this-weekend/ |title=NYT publisher marries his girlfriend |date=August 30, 2014 |accessdate=November 21, 2014}}</ref> Sulzberger has a son, Arthur Gregg,<ref>[http://www.observer.com/2009/media/2009-ag-arthur-gregg-sulzberger-era-begins Arthur Gregg Sulzberger]</ref> and a daughter, Annie; both attended [[Brown University]] in [[Providence, Rhode Island]]. Arthur Gregg Sulzberger began writing for ''The New York Times'' in February 2009.{{cn|date=March 2015}}\n\nSulzberger became engaged to Gabrielle Greene in May 2014,<ref name=pagesix /> and married on August 30, 2014.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.nytimes.com/2014/08/31/fashion/weddings/gabrielle-greene-and-arthur-sulzberger-jr.html?_r=1 |title=Gabrielle Greene and Arthur Sulzberger Jr.\n |date=August 31, 2014 |accessdate=November 21, 2014}}</ref>\n\n==Awards and honors==\n*2012 [[National Book Award]], [[Literarian Award for Outstanding Service to the American Literary Community]]<ref name=nyt2012>{{cite web |url=http://www.nytimes.com/2012/11/15/us/louise-erdrichs-novel-the-round-house-wins-national-book-award.html |title=Novel About Racial Injustice Wins National Book Award |work=[[New York Times]] |author=Leslie Kaufman |date=November 14, 2012 |accessdate=November 15, 2012}}</ref>\n\n==References==\n{{Reflist}}\n\n==Sources==\n*Bair, Callen.
